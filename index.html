Abstract

Many high-stakes applications of agentic AI‚Äîe.g., scientific discovery and medical diagnosis‚Äîrequire forming hypotheses and running targeted experiments. Given limited resources, to what extent do agents act rationally, and can insights from human behavior make them more efficient information-seekers? We introduce a decision-oriented dialogue task that provides a lightweight evaluation harness for Bayesian Experimental Design (BED).

In our cognitively-inspired *Collaborative Battleship* game, a partially-informed *Captain* must balance exploration (asking questions) and action (taking shots), while a fully-informed *Spotter* must provide grounded answers under an information bottleneck. Experiments with human players (N=42) and language model (LM) agents reveal gaps in reasoning about answer context, generating informative questions, and selecting high-value actions. We propose Bayes-rational strategies that translate questions into Python for Monte Carlo inference.

For Spotter agents, this approach boosts accuracy by up to 14.7% absolute over LM-only baselines; for Captain agents, it raises expected information gain (EIG) by up to 0.227 bits (94.2% of the achievable noise ceiling). Combined with a maximum a posteriori action policy, these components yield sharper targeting (+0.303‚Äì0.374 F1) and enable weaker LMs to attain strong Captain performance against humans (78‚Äì81% win rate) and frontier models (59‚Äì62% vs. GPT-5). Finally, we generalize our approach to an open-domain setting based on Twenty Questions. Overall, our work contributes a novel dataset and benchmark for BED and multiple inference-time strategies for building rational agents in discovery settings.
